* Preface: Why These Books Together
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: preface
:END:

These seven texts are not an arbitrary syllabus.  Read together, they
trace a single arc: /how mathematical knowledge grows through
conjecture, proof, refutation, and progressive generalization/.

Pólya supplies the heuristic engine---the art of plausible reasoning
that gets you to a conjecture in the first place.  Lakatos shows what
happens /after/ the conjecture: the dialectical cycle of
proof-attempts and counterexamples that refines both theorem and
concept simultaneously.  The five calculus texts then instantiate this
pattern across increasingly general domains, each one extending the
last by relaxing assumptions the predecessor took for granted.

The =guile-*= projects formalize these ideas computationally---making
the dialectic auditable, the heuristics executable, and the
generalizations type-checked.

* Common Themes

** Theme 1: Conjecture → Proof → Refutation → Refinement

The Lakatos cycle is the meta-pattern unifying everything here.

- *Pólya* codifies /Phase 1/: generating conjectures through analogy,
  specialization, and generalization.
- *Lakatos* codifies /Phases 2–4/: the social and logical process by
  which proofs are stress-tested and concepts stretched.
- Each calculus text enacts this cycle within its own domain:
  - Thomas & Finney establish the "naive conjecture" of classical
    calculus (continuous, well-behaved functions).
  - Davis refutes it: Fourier series converge for functions that
    classical calculus can barely describe.
  - Gelfand & Fomin generalize the object of study from numbers to
    /functionals/, refuting the assumption that calculus operates on
    finite-dimensional inputs.
  - Erdélyi refutes the demand for convergence itself, showing that
    divergent series carry precise asymptotic information.
  - Oldham & Spanier refute the assumption that differentiation order
    must be integer, completing the generalization.

Each level /subsumes/ rather than replaces the one below it:
- The classical derivative is a special case of the spectral derivative.
- The spectral derivative is a special case of the functional derivative.
- Asymptotic methods give meaning to formal operations the others reject.
- The fractional derivative unifies integer-order differentiation and
  integration into a single continuous operator.

** Theme 3: Heuristics as First-Class Objects

Pólya's heuristics---analogy, generalization, specialization,
decomposition---are not mere pedagogical advice.  They recur as
/structural operations/ in every text:

| Heuristic          | Thomas & Finney    | Davis              | Gelfand & Fomin       | Erdélyi               | Oldham & Spanier       |
|--------------------+--------------------+--------------------+-----------------------+-----------------------+------------------------|
| *Analogy*          | Derivative ↔ slope | Time ↔ frequency   | Path ↔ function space | Convergent ↔ formal   | Integer ↔ real order   |
| *Generalization*   | Single → multi-var | Periodic → L²      | Finite → infinite dim | Convergent → divergent | ℤ-order → ℝ-order     |
| *Specialization*   | Limit definitions  | Fourier → Taylor   | Euler–Lagrange eqn    | Stirling's formula    | Γ(n+1) = n!            |
| *Decomposition*    | Partial fractions  | Orthogonal basis   | Necessary conditions  | Saddle-point method   | Riemann–Liouville def  |

** Theme 4: The Tension Between Rigor and Discovery

Every one of these authors grapples with the same methodological
tension:

- *Pólya*: "Mathematics presented with rigor is a systematic deductive
  science, but mathematics /in the making/ is an experimental
  inductive science."
- *Lakatos*: Formalism kills the dialectic; but informalism permits
  error.  The resolution is /progressive formalization/.
- *Davis*: Fourier's original arguments were non-rigorous but correct;
  Dirichlet's rigorization changed the meaning of "function."
- *Gelfand & Fomin*: Variational problems were solved by physicists
  long before analysts made them precise.
- *Erdélyi*: Divergent series "work" in practice; the theory of
  asymptotic expansions explains /why/ without demanding convergence.
- *Oldham & Spanier*: Fractional derivatives were conjectured by
  Leibniz in 1695; rigorous foundations took 300 years.

This tension maps directly onto the =guile-par= / elenctic-spec
methodology: start informal (L0), harden progressively (L1–L3), and
preserve the dialectical audit trail.

* Recommended Reading Order

** Phase 0: Methodology (Read First, Return Often)

#+begin_quote
Read these before touching any calculus text.  They change /how/ you
read mathematics, not just /what/ you read.
#+end_quote

1. *Pólya, /How to Solve It/* — Internalize the heuristic vocabulary:
   analogy, generalization, specialization, auxiliary elements,
   working backwards.  This is the language you will use to navigate
   every subsequent text.

2. *Lakatos, /Proofs and Refutations/* — Understand that definitions
   are not given but /negotiated/ through the proof-refutation cycle.
   This reframes every "definition" in the calculus texts as a
   provisional settlement, not a decree.

** Phase 1: The Classical Foundation

3. [@3] *Thomas & Finney, /Calculus & Analytic Geometry/ (=guile-cag=)* —
   Establish fluency with limits, derivatives, integrals, and series
   in their standard setting.  Pay attention to where the authors
   /restrict/ hypotheses (continuity, differentiability, uniform
   convergence)---these are the monster-barring moves Lakatos warned
   you about.

** Phase 2: The First Generalization (Spectral)

4. [@4] *Davis, /Fourier Series & Orthogonal Functions/ (=guile-fsof=)* —
   The transition from pointwise to spectral thinking.  Here the
   Lakatos cycle fires immediately: Fourier claimed any function could
   be expanded; Dirichlet showed this fails without conditions; the
   concept of "function" itself was refined.  Requires solid
   integration theory from Phase 1.

** Phase 3: The Variational Turn

5. [@5] *Gelfand & Fomin, /Calculus of Variations/ (=guile-cov=)* —
   The domain of the derivative shifts from numbers to entire
   functions.  The Euler–Lagrange equation is the "derivative = 0"
   condition lifted to infinite-dimensional space.  Fourier methods
   from Phase 2 appear naturally in eigenvalue problems for
   differential operators.

** Phase 4: Beyond Convergence

6. [@6] *Erdélyi, /Asymptotic Expansions/ (=guile-ae=)* — Requires
   comfort with series from Phase 1 and special functions encountered
   in Phases 2–3.  The key conceptual leap: a divergent series can
   carry more useful information than a convergent one.  This is a
   Lakatos-style refutation of the deepest assumption in classical
   analysis.

** Phase 5: The Final Generalization

7. [@7] *Oldham & Spanier, /Fractional Calculus/ (=guile-fc=)* — Read
   last.  It presupposes everything: classical calculus, Fourier
   transforms (for defining fractional operators spectrally), the
   Gamma function and special functions (from asymptotics), and
   functional-analytic thinking (from variations).  The fractional
   derivative is the capstone generalization that unifies
   differentiation and integration into a single parameterized
   operator.

* Connections to the Guile Programme

** The =guile-par= Thread

Every =guile-*= project should maintain a =par-log=: a structured
record of conjectures attempted, counterexamples found, and concept
refinements made during development.  This is the computational
instantiation of Lakatos's method.

#+begin_src scheme :tangle no
;; Sketch: a par-log entry
(define-record-type <par-entry>
  (make-par-entry id conjecture proof-attempt counterexample resolution)
  par-entry?

  (conjecture     par-entry-conjecture)
  (proof-attempt  par-entry-proof-attempt)
  (counterexample par-entry-counterexample)
  (resolution     par-entry-resolution))  ; 'monster-bar | 'exception-bar | 'lemma-incorporate
#+end_src
